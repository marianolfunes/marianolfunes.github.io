Vaca Muerta needs $125–170B of execution.

Where the cost gap to the Permian actually closes.
Mariano Funes — 25 years procurement leadership in Argentine oil & gas (Director-level at Shell, ENAP Sipetrol, Archer, San Antonio Internacional). Currently building Procurement OS on Anthropic Managed Agents. May 2026

In May 2026, McKinsey published the case for Vaca Muerta at scale — $125 to $170 billion of investment across the value chain over the next decade, up to five percent of Argentine GDP by 2030, oil production approaching the Permian's mid-2010s ramp.1 The numbers are right. The piece that follows works from the same numbers and asks a different question — what does execution at this scale actually require, and where does the cost gap to the Permian actually close?

What the McKinsey lens shows well

The McKinsey article is good at what it sets out to do. It frames the size of the prize for capital allocators, lays out the four interconnected segments that have to move together — upstream, midstream, export, domestic consumption — and translates a basin geology question into a coordinated capital-architecture question. The demand side is grounded in their own LNG buyer survey: flexibility, supplier reliability, and emissions transparency are now the contract terms global buyers prize most.2 The capex breakdown by segment ($112–126B upstream, $13–22B midstream, $2–20B international export) gives a board-level reader a clean view of where capital has to go.

If you are an investor, a policymaker, or an executive sitting on a board that needs a one-paper case for Vaca Muerta, the McKinsey piece does its job.

What the operator lens adds

The operator works from the same numbers and asks a different question. Not "what is the size of the prize?" but "who actually executes this, and what does that execution look like in practice?" Different optimization target, different things visible.

Three layers come into view through that lens that the consultant lens, by design, abstracts away: the cost-gap arithmetic broken into named drivers, the contractor-labor-operator dynamic that is specific to Argentine oil & gas, and the multi-tier supplier-and-people reality that sits on top of every piece of equipment in McKinsey's charts.

The cost gap, with McKinsey's own arithmetic

McKinsey is direct about the cost differential. On the well economics:

Currently, average costs for a well can range from $12 million to $16 million for a 2,800-meter well. When normalizing for geology, this is 30 to 40 percent more expensive than comparable wells in the Permian Basin in the United States. Without narrowing this gap, Argentina risks structural disadvantage in global LNG markets. McKinsey, page 7

Then, eleven pages later, they break the gap into one named component:

Upgrading road and rail networks could reduce the high costs of moving rigs, sand, and equipment — currently accounting for up to 20 to 30 percent of Argentina's 35 percent cost gap with the Permian Basin in the United States. McKinsey, page 11

Do the arithmetic. Logistics — road, rail, long-haul trucking — accounts for 20 to 30 percentage points of a 35-point gap. The residual is 5 to 15 percentage points. McKinsey names the logistics component. They do not break out the residual, and that is where the operator's view has more to say.

What the residual contains

The 5 to 15 percentage points that McKinsey does not break out is not a single thing. It is a stack of factors that are specific to operating in Argentina rather than the Permian, and that an operator running procurement at scale spends most of their time on.

Local labor regimes. Shift structures, manning ratios, overtime treatment, and work-rule rigidity all differ between Argentine oil & gas and US shale. The implications for daily cost are real and operator-specific.

Environmental application at provincial level. Water handling, flaring, well-abandonment standards, and community / social-license requirements are administered at provincial level in Neuquén and Río Negro. The cadence of permitting and the cost of compliance translate into project schedules and well economics in ways that aggregate-national framing misses.

Financing friction. The Argentine country-risk premium, the mechanics of USD-peso conversion, and capital repatriation rules shape contract structures in ways that a Permian-trained service company has never had to design around.

None of these are McKinsey's blind spot. They are below the abstraction level the McKinsey piece operates at. The piece that follows brings them up.

The contractor-labor-operator nexus

The single thing that distinguishes Vaca Muerta procurement from the Permian.

Argentine labor law puts the responsibility for a contractor's employees on the contractor itself. As of the June 2024 Ley Bases reform, the operator can be freed from solidary responsibility by maintaining a specific documentation set for every contractor's worker — CUIL number, social security contributions, remuneration records, salary bank account, ART coverage with endorsement in the operator's favor.3 The reform tightened the documentation burden; it also offered a real legal exemption.

That is the legal frame. The operational reality runs differently.

Argentine oil & gas unions count most of the workers servicing a site as belonging to the site operator — regardless of whose payroll those workers sit on, and regardless of the new legal exemption. The union's posture toward the operator absorbs every supplier and contractor's labor force into a single bargaining surface. This is how Argentine oil & gas labor relations work structurally, sector-wide.

For a Vaca Muerta operator, two things follow. Every contractor negotiation is also a labor-relations decision. And every supplier rate at the site includes the labor risk that the operator is backstopping in practice — not just legally.

This is the layer that a 200-rig redeployment from the Permian runs into the moment it tries to clear customs. In the Permian, a US operator contracts with a service company and the service company's workforce is the service company's problem. Clean separation, market-based. In Vaca Muerta, an Argentine operator contracts with that same service company and inherits the labor exposure of its workforce, because the union does not recognize the legal separation. There is no clean line. The procurement function is co-managing labor across the multi-tier supplier network as a matter of operational practice — work that doesn't exist in the Permian operator's playbook.

The Milei reform tightened this rather than loosened it. The operator now carries more procurement-side compliance work (a documentation regime across every contractor's workforce, which is itself a system-design problem at multi-tier supplier scale) and the same union exposure. The legal-versus-practical gap widened, not closed. McKinsey's "labor mobility" framing on page 11 — bringing in international experts via streamlined permitting — does not address any of this.

The supplier layer that does not appear in the charts

McKinsey's exhibits 3, 4, and 5 enumerate equipment (rigs, frac pumping units, coiled tubing, proppant), facilities (gas plants, fractionators, pipelines), and end markets. The charts are clean and accurate for what they are designed to show. What they do not show is the layer that sits on top of every piece of that equipment: the service companies that operate it, the people who staff those companies, the contracts that bind them, and the management systems that hold the whole thing together.

Scale-up multipliers help make this visible. McKinsey projects high-spec drilling rigs growing 1.7×, frac pumping units 2.5×, coiled tubing units 2.3×, proppant 2.6× by 2030.4 Each of those multipliers translates into more crews, more supervisors, more training pipelines, more spare-parts logistics, more in-country offices, more dispatch coordination, more local-labor relations — none of which is visible in the chart, and all of which has to be procured.

The 200-plus idle high-spec rigs in North America that McKinsey describes as a "redeployment and technology transfer opportunity" is, from the procurement side, a multi-tier commercial-structuring problem. Each rig brings with it the service company that runs it, the labor force that staffs it, the supplier ecosystem behind it (drilling fluids, cementing, directional, wireline, frac, tubulars, transport, camp, fuel), and the management system that has to translate Permian operating expectations into Argentine operational reality. Procurement does that work.

Where execution-level value gets created

If the 30 to 40 percent cost gap to the Permian is going to close, it does not close in drilling tech. Argentine wells use the same rigs, the same fluids, the same completions methods as the Permian. The gap closes in procurement-side levers: supplier consolidation, contract structures that distribute risk where it belongs, performance-based pricing, sustained supplier development, expediting discipline across the multi-tier supplier network, and total-cost-of-ownership analysis applied category by category.

This is the layer that an operator who has run procurement at $150–300M of annual spend across Argentine oil & gas has seen up close. It is also the layer where a few hundred basis points add up to billions when applied at $125–170B of programme spend over a decade.

Agentic procurement infrastructure at this scale

The procurement function at Vaca Muerta scale is going to change. Not because of any single piece of technology, but because the volume of contracting, supplier qualification, performance management, and compliance documentation that the next decade requires cannot be handled by the headcount-and-spreadsheets pattern that currently runs Argentine oil & gas procurement.

Agentic systems built on the current generation of language models — Claude Managed Agents shipped in April 2026, the parallel infrastructure from other foundation labs — change the math on what one procurement organization can execute. Supplier qualification velocity, contract administration scale, performance management cadence, the Article 30 documentation regime itself — each of these is a candidate for an agent-mediated workflow that an operator with domain depth can specify and direct.

This is what Procurement OS — the system I am building — operates on. It is not the subject of this piece; it is the artifact behind the perspective it operates from.

Two questions, both right

McKinsey asks: what is the size of the prize, and how is it coordinated? Their answer is good. $125 to $170 billion across the value chain over the next decade, with a competitive investment environment, a built-up local talent base, and an upgraded logistics backbone as the three coordinating actions.

The operator's complementary question is: who actually executes this, and what does that execution look like in practice? It looks like a procurement function operating across a multi-tier supplier network, co-managing labor exposure that does not separate the way the legal frame suggests, navigating provincial environmental application, structuring contracts around financing friction the Permian never has to think about — and building the infrastructure to handle that volume at scale.

Both questions matter. The basin will close the gap to the Permian in the answers to the second one.
